# 📘 Lesson 7 — Institutional Supply & Demand Zones

---

## 🎯 Objective

Learn how to identify **Institutional Supply & Demand Zones**, understand **why they form**, how to **draw them correctly**, and determine whether a zone is still valid for trading.

---

# 📖 What are Supply & Demand Zones?

Supply and Demand Zones are areas where **institutions placed large buy or sell orders**, causing price to move aggressively.

These zones represent the **footprints of institutional activity** and may attract price again when unfilled orders remain.

---

## 🟢 Demand Zone

A **Demand Zone** is an area where **strong buying pressure** entered the market.

**Result:**

- Buyers overwhelmed sellers.
    
- Price moved sharply upward.
    

> 💡 **Think of it as an area where institutions accumulated buy orders.**

---

## 🔴 Supply Zone

A **Supply Zone** is an area where **strong selling pressure** entered the market.

**Result:**

- Sellers overwhelmed buyers.
    
- Price moved sharply downward.
    

> 💡 **Think of it as an area where institutions distributed sell orders.**

---

# 🔍 How to Identify Supply & Demand Zones

Follow these three steps:

### 1️⃣ Find an Imbalance

Look for a **strong impulsive move** away from a price area.

Characteristics:

- Large momentum candles
    
- Strong directional movement
    
- Clear imbalance between buyers and sellers
    

---

### 2️⃣ Find the Origin

Locate **where the strong move began**.

This origin becomes your **Supply** or **Demand Zone**.

---

### 3️⃣ Evaluate the Strength

The more aggressive the move away from the zone:

- Higher institutional participation
    
- Stronger zone
    
- Greater importance
    

> 💡 Strong departures usually indicate stronger institutional order flow.

---

# 📐 Methods for Drawing Zones

There are **two ways** to draw Supply & Demand Zones.

---

## 📦 Method 1 — Range Method

Draw the zone using the **entire consolidation** that occurred before the impulsive move.

### Steps

1. Identify the consolidation.
    
2. Mark the highest point.
    
3. Mark the lowest point.
    
4. Draw a box covering the entire range.
    

### ✅ Advantages

- Larger zone
    
- Higher probability of price revisiting the area
    

### ❌ Disadvantages

- Lower Risk-to-Reward ratio
    
- Less precise entries
    

---

## 🎯 Method 2 — Pivot Method

Draw the zone using the **single pivot candle** that initiated the impulsive move.

### Steps

1. Identify the pivot candle.
    
2. Mark the entire candle as the zone.
    

### ✅ Advantages

- More precise entries
    
- Higher Risk-to-Reward ratio
    

### ❌ Disadvantages

- Smaller zone
    
- Greater chance of missing the trade if price doesn't retrace deep enough
    

---

# ⚖️ Range Method vs. Pivot Method

|Feature|📦 Range Method|🎯 Pivot Method|
|---|---|---|
|Zone Size|Larger|Smaller|
|Precision|Lower|Higher|
|Risk-to-Reward|Lower|Higher|
|Chance of Entry|Higher|Lower|

---

# 🔄 Mitigation

## 📖 What is Mitigation?

Mitigation occurs when **price returns to a Supply or Demand Zone** to fill remaining institutional orders.

---

## ✅ Valid Mitigation Rule

A zone is considered **mitigated** only when price reaches **at least 50% of the zone**.

> 📌 **50% penetration is the instructor's mechanical rule for mitigation.**

---

## 🟢 Unmitigated Zone

Price has **not reached the 50% level**.

These zones remain active and may attract price in the future.

---

## 🔴 Mitigated Zone

Price has already reached **50% or deeper** into the zone.

These zones are considered weaker, and future price is more likely to move through them.

---

# 📋 Trading Rules

### ✅ Don't Trade Every Zone

A Supply or Demand Zone alone is **not** enough to justify a trade.

Always combine it with:

- Market Structure
    
- Trend Direction
    
- Liquidity
    
- Entry Confirmation
    

---

### 📈 Trade With the Trend

Example:

- Bullish Market → Focus on **Demand Zones**
    
- Bearish Market → Focus on **Supply Zones**
    

> ⚠️ Avoid selling at weak highs during a bullish trend simply because a Supply Zone exists.

---

### ⏳ Wait for Confirmation

Never enter a trade immediately after price reaches a zone.

Wait for confirmation such as:

- Internal Market Structure Shift
    
- Liquidity Sweep
    
- Other entry models taught later in the course
    

---

# ⚠️ Common Mistakes

❌ Treating every Supply or Demand Zone as a trade opportunity.

❌ Ignoring the overall market trend.

❌ Entering without confirmation.

❌ Forgetting to check whether the zone has already been mitigated.

---

# 💡 Pro Tips

- Strong impulsive moves usually create stronger institutional zones.
    
- Unmitigated zones generally have greater significance than mitigated ones.
    
- The Pivot Method offers precision, while the Range Method offers higher probability of participation.
    
- The ability to identify quality zones improves through repeated chart practice.
    

---

# ⭐ Key Takeaways

- Supply & Demand Zones represent institutional buying and selling activity.
    
- Identify zones by finding **strong imbalances** and marking their origin.
    
- Draw zones using either the **Range Method** or the **Pivot Method**.
    
- A zone is considered mitigated only after price reaches **50%** of it.
    
- Never trade a zone in isolation—always consider trend, structure, liquidity, and confirmation.
